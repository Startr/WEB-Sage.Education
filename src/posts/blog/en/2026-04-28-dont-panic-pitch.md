# Article Pitch — Revised Workshop Draft
## "Don't Panic: How a Girls' School Stopped Debating AI and Started Building With It"

**Series:** Sage.Education
**Cross-publication:** community newspapers, local news websites, ed-tech press
**Based on:** Amalia Liogas's presentation at the Apple Education Event, May 4, 2026
**Timing:** Published after the event (post-May 4)
**Voice:** First-person plural ("we") from The Study
**Target length:** 2,800–3,500 words
**Byline:** The Study, Westmount, Montreal (with Amalia Liogas named in-text as Director of IT)

---

## Why This Article, Why Now

On May 4, 2026, Amalia Liogas and two students from The Study presented at Apple's education event. They told a room of IT and pedagogy directors how a small all-girls' school in Westmount built its own AI infrastructure — and then got out of the way so its students could build on top of it.

That talk was for insiders. This article is the public version — written for the parents, policymakers, school boards, and community members who need to hear the same argument but from a different angle.

The timing is deliberate. Canada is actively debating age-gating AI chatbots. The federal and provincial conversation treats young people as passive consumers of AI who need to be shielded from it. The Study's experience offers a counter-narrative grounded in evidence, not ideology: students are not just consumers of AI — they are builders, and the schools that recognize this will define the next era of education.

## Structure

### 1. Cold Open — The Wrong Question (~300 words)

*Sets the scene. Positions the article against the current Canadian policy moment.*

We open not at the Apple event but at The Study itself — a brief, grounded image of the school. Then we name the question most institutions are stuck on: *Should AI be in our school?* We explain why we stopped asking it eighteen months ago. While adults debated whether AI belonged in education, our students were already using it. The real question was never *whether* — it was *whose AI, on whose terms, in whose hands.*

For the broader audience, we connect this directly to the Canadian age-blocking debate: the impulse to gate young people away from AI assumes they are passive recipients. Our experience says otherwise.

**Key line to land:** *"We did not wait for permission. We did not wait for policy. We asked a different question: what happens when girls build it themselves?"*

---

### 2. The Shift — From Information to Imagination (~500 words)

*The conceptual frame. This is the section that gives the article intellectual weight beyond a school profile.*

The schools we run today were designed for the Information Age. Their job was access — getting students near books, data, experts. That job is done. Information is now infinite, instant, and free.

The scarce resource of the next century is not information. It is imagination — the ability to look at infinite information and make something that did not exist before. This is the Imagination Age. It has already started.

We expand beyond what a 75-second talk segment can do. We give parents and policymakers a framework they can carry into other conversations:

- The Information Age rewarded retrieval. The Imagination Age rewards creation.
- AI is not the enemy of learning. It is the catalyst that forces education to become what it should have been all along — not the storage of facts, but the practice of creation.
- Schools that respond to this shift by banning tools are optimizing for an era that is already over.

**Key line to land:** *"The question is not whether our students will use AI. The question is whether they will use it as consumers — or as creators."*

---

### 3. The Foundation We Built — Meet Rosie (~400 words)

*The infrastructure story. Concrete enough for IT directors and school boards; clear enough for parents.*

We built our own AI. Her name is Rosie. She lives on our campus, on our infrastructure, and she was built in partnership with Sage.is.

Three things matter about Rosie:
- **Sovereign:** On-site. Our data stays with us. No student data leaves campus.
- **Transparent:** Teachers shape what she does. She is not a black box.
- **Liberating:** Rosie is the foundation that lets our students freely use everything else. The point of owning your foundation is that you stop having to fight about the floor above it.

We make the architecture argument plainly for a parent audience: owning the base layer means we don't have to say no to every new tool. We can say yes — because the floor is ours.

**Key line to land:** *"We did not build Rosie so our students would have to use her. We built Rosie so our students would never have to be locked into anything else."*

---

### 4. What the Girls Built — SafeTrace (~500 words)

*The proof. This is where the student block quotes live. This section is the emotional centre of the article.*

We introduce SafeTrace: an AI tool that detects self-doxxing in photos. Upload a photo — the kind of photo a teenager might post — and SafeTrace tells you what you've accidentally revealed. Your school crest. The street sign in the background. The reflection in a window. Built by students, for students, to address a problem that girls face disproportionately.

The students built it on Lovable. Other students at The Study build on Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, or directly with models running on Rosie. We do not dictate the stack.

**Student block quotes (from post-event interviews):**
- Why they built it — the personal moment that made the problem real
- Which tool they chose and why
- What surprised them
- What they want adults to understand about how their generation uses AI
- What they would build next if no one stopped them

*[Note: These quotes will be gathered from actual student interviews. The article will include 3–5 direct quotes, each attributed by first name only with parental consent confirmed.]*

**Key line to land:** *"The people best positioned to solve the problems girls face online turned out to be girls — with the right tools and the freedom to choose them."*

---

### 5. The Pattern — Tool Fluency, Not Tool Loyalty (~350 words)

*The pedagogical argument. This is the section that distinguishes The Study's approach from "we bought an AI product."*

Last year's class built remarkable things on Rosie — and then graduated. That is how schools work. The projects do not compound the way they do in a company. What compounds is the posture.

Tool fluency means: the students you read about above will use a tool next year that does not exist yet. The class after them will replace it again. This is what Imagination Age literacy looks like. Not loyalty to a platform. The ability to choose one — fluently, fearlessly, on the merits.

For the policy audience, we draw the contrast explicitly: age-gating AI treats tools as threats to be managed. Tool fluency treats tools as instruments to be mastered. The difference in outcome is the difference between a generation of cautious consumers and a generation of confident creators.

**Key line to land:** *"The girls will replace every tool in this article before they graduate. Good."*

---

### 6. What You Can Do — The Playbook (~400 words)

*Actionable. Three moves, adapted for the broader audience — not just IT directors but parents, board members, community leaders.*

**One: Own your foundation. Free everything above it.**
Your school's AI strategy has two layers. The foundation — institutional AI, data governance, operational infrastructure — should be sovereign. Everything above it — the tools students choose for their own projects — should be open. Your job is not to pick the tool. Your job is to build the floor solid enough that students can stand on it and imagine anything.

**Two: Let students build, with the tools they pick.**
The most powerful curriculum is the one students write themselves. The Imagination Age does not reward students who memorized the right answer. It rewards students who can ask a better question.

**Three: Make girls' voices the default, not the diversity slide.**
The Information Age was built — mostly — by men. The Imagination Age is being built right now. The next decade of AI will be shaped by the people in the room when it is made. Whatever school you support, whatever board you sit on — the question is not whether your girls are interested in AI. They are. The question is whether you have built a place where they can imagine with it.

---

### 7. About Sage (~150 words)

*Short, factual, positioned as infrastructure context — not a pitch.*

Rosie runs on infrastructure built by Sage.is, a Canadian AI company whose platform enables schools and organizations to deploy sovereign AI — on their own terms, on their own hardware, with their own data. Sage's approach to AI mirrors what we believe about education: the foundation should be owned, transparent, and liberating.

Sage.Education is the education-focused arm of this work — a growing series of case studies, frameworks, and community stories about what happens when schools stop debating AI and start building with it. This article is part of that series. Not because Sage built what our students built — they didn't. Because the question of who controls the foundation of AI in education is one every school, parent, and policymaker will face. We think our answer is worth sharing.

---

### 8. The Close — 42 (~200 words)

*Callback to the presentation. The emotional landing.*

There is a number on our school's project site — 42. It comes from *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*: 42 is the answer to life, the universe, and everything. The joke is that no one ever figured out the question.

The Information Age was the same. We spent a century gathering answers — the tools, the models, the infrastructure. We have them now. The question is what we will imagine with them.

We think the people most likely to find that question are the ones in our classrooms right now, building things we did not ask them to build, choosing tools we did not choose for them, solving problems we had not thought to name.

Don't panic. The future is in good hands.

**Key line to land:** *"The answer is 42. We're still working on the question."*

## Tone Guide

- **Confident, not defensive.** We are not justifying our approach. We are sharing it.
- **Plain, not jargon-heavy.** A parent in Westmount and a policy adviser in Ottawa should both finish the article.
- **Warm, not corporate.** This is a school talking about its students, not a company talking about its product.
- **Specific, not abstract.** Rosie has a name. SafeTrace has a function. The students have voices.
- **The Sage paragraph is matter-of-fact.** One paragraph. Infrastructure partner. Part of a series. No superlatives.

---

## Things This Article Does NOT Do

- Does not use "AI revolution" language
- Does not fear-monger about what other schools are or aren't doing
- Does not position The Study as exceptional — positions the model as transferable
- Does not read as a vendor pitch for Sage (Sage gets one contextual paragraph)
- Does not apologize for being a small school
- Does not talk down to students or treat them as props in an adult story
- Does not take a partisan position on Canadian AI policy — but does offer a clear counter-narrative to age-gating as the sole strategy

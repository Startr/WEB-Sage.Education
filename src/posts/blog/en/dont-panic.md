On a residential street in Westmount, Montreal, there is school where Grade 8 students wire their own lighting systems, where kindergartners learn to code, and where a team of teenage girls recently built an AI tool that detects when a photograph reveals more about the person who posted it than they intended. The school is called The Study, an independent bilingual all-girls' school founded in 1915. It has roughly 400 students. And it is the only K-12 institution in Canada to have developed and deployed its own private AI agent.

The agent's name is Rosie, after both the robot from The Jetsons and Rosie the Riveter, the wartime icon of women doing work that the world said was not for them. She runs on campus, on The Study's own hardware, and no student data leaves the building. But Rosie is not the story. The story is what happened next: teachers and girls started building their own AI agents and tools on top of her.

This is not the behaviour that Canadian policymakers expected from teenage girls and AI.

## The Wrong Question

In April 2026, Canada's federal Liberal Party voted to back a minimum age of sixteen for social media accounts and AI chatbots.[^1] Manitoba announced it would be the first province to implement the ban, starting in schools.[^2] Culture Minister Marc Miller said the government was "seriously" considering a national ban or moratorium.[^3] A May 2026 poll found that more than two-thirds of Canadians supported restricting access to AI chatbots for children under sixteen.[^4]

The impulse is understandable. The execution is not.

Michael Geist, the University of Ottawa law professor who has become English Canada's most prominent voice on digital policy, called the push "the illusion of protection." A ban, Geist argued, lets platforms off the hook for what actually causes harm, forces millions of Canadians into invasive age verification systems, and pushes young people toward offshore AI services with no content moderation, no safety filters, and no crisis intervention routing.[^5] The technology to enforce such a ban in a privacy-protective manner, he noted, does not yet exist.

Meanwhile, the students are already using it. A KPMG survey published in October 2025 found that 73 percent of Canadian students relied on generative AI for their schoolwork, up from 52 percent two years earlier.[^6] Daily use had jumped 15 percent in a single year. Prohibition has not worked. Three years into the frenzy, teachers who spoke to The Logic said they were warming to the idea of AI in the classroom, and some said ignoring it posed a greater danger than engaging with it.[^7]

> **The question most schools are asking is whether AI should be allowed in. The Study stopped asking that question eighteen months ago. The question it asks instead is: whose AI, on whose terms, in whose hands.**

The answer it arrived at was not brave or radical. It was the natural next step in a decades-long trajectory.

## From Information to Imagination

![[painting-schoolmistress-chardin.jpg]]
*Jean-Baptiste-Siméon Chardin, "The Schoolmistress" (c. 1735). National Gallery, London. One teaches, the other learns. The tools are simple. The method is what matters. Public domain*

The schools most institutions run today were designed for the Information Age. Their job was access: getting students near books, data, experts. That job is done. Information is now infinite, instant, and free. The scarce resource of the next era is not information. It is imagination – the ability to look at infinite information and make something that did not exist before.

The Study, an all-girls' school with 400 students, has been practicing this for longer than most schools have been debating it.

The Study's own AI School Framework describes a "deliberate and strategic approach to educational technology integration" spanning decades.[^8] The school was the first in Quebec to implement a one-to-one laptop program. Coding is taught from kindergarten. Robotics begins in Grade 4. Design thinking methodology is embedded from K through 11: students iterate, experiment, and learn through failure within a structured and supportive environment. The Framework's stated philosophy is that "students should engage with technology as creators rather than passive consumers."

This philosophy predates AI by years. Students at The Study have built working go-karts. They have prototyped startups that were recognized at national and international competitions. They have designed and wired their own lighting systems in Grade 8. They have presented research with real-world solutions at the United Nations Commission on the Status of Women. They have created digital games driven entirely by their own interests and initiatives.

When generative AI arrived, it did not change The Study's philosophy. It walked into a building that was already built for it.

The Framework states that AI will be approached "not as a novelty, but as a powerful tool for innovation, agency, and meaningful impact." In practice, that meant building Rosie.

## Meet Rosie

[PHOTO – Rosie interface or students working with Rosie. To be sourced from Amalia/The Study with permission.]

Rosie is a private AI agent deployed on The Study's campus infrastructure through [Sage.is](https://sage.is)'s AI-UI platform. She is named after two icons: the robot from The Jetsons, a character who was part of the household rather than a replacement for it, and Rosie the Riveter, the image of a woman rolling up her sleeves to do the work. At an all-girls' school, the name carries weight.

Four things matter about Rosie.

Rosie is **sovereign**. She runs on-site. Student data stays on campus. Nothing leaves the building. In an era when most school AI tools route student queries through third-party servers in jurisdictions with different privacy laws, The Study's students interact with an AI that their school owns and controls.

Every aspect of Rosie is **transparent**. Teachers shape what she does. She is not a black box. The curriculum team decides how she is used, what guardrails are in place, and how her capabilities evolve. This is not a product that arrived with a terms-of-service agreement and a prayer. It is infrastructure that the school governs.

The platform is **liberating**. Rosie is the foundation that lets students freely use everything else. When the base layer is owned, the school does not have to say no to every new tool that appears. It can say yes – because the floor is solid.

Most importantly, Rosie is **generative**. This is the part that most schools have not considered. Rosie is not the end product. She is the launchpad. Teachers and students are learning to build their own AI agents and tools on top of Rosie's platform – creating custom solutions for problems they identify themselves. The distinction is the difference between giving students a calculator and giving them a workshop.

## What the Girls Built

![[painting-anguissola-self-portrait.jpg]]
*Sofonisba Anguissola, "Self-Portrait at the Easel" (1556). Łańcut Castle, Poland. A woman at work with her own tools, on her own terms. Five centuries later, the posture is the same. Public domain*

[PHOTO – Students at the Apple event or working on SafeTrace. Parental consent required.]

On May 4, 2026, Amalia Liogas, The Study's Director of IT, and a team of students stood in front of a room of IT directors and pedagogy leads at Apple's education event. They were not there to talk about using AI. They were there to talk about building with it.

CTV had covered The Study's AI initiative a few weeks before the event.[^9] After the presentation, two private schools reached out to Amalia asking to see Rosie. A McGill University professor reached out about teaching vibe coding. The interest was not in the technology. It was in the posture: an all-girls' school that treats its students as builders, not users.

The centrepiece of the presentation was SafeTrace: an AI tool that detects self-doxxing in photographs. Upload a photo (the kind of photo a teenager might post to social media) and SafeTrace identifies what you have accidentally revealed. Your school crest on a blazer. A street sign reflected in a window. A GPS coordinate embedded in the metadata. Built by students, for students, to address a problem that girls face disproportionately.

The students built SafeTrace on Lovable. Other students at The Study build on Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, or directly with models running on Rosie – where they are learning to create their own AI agents and tools. The school does not dictate the stack. It provides the platform and the method. The students provide the imagination.

## Tool Fluency, Not Tool Loyalty

![[painting-leyster-self-portrait.jpg]]
*Judith Leyster, "Self-Portrait" (c. 1630). National Gallery of Art, Washington. She chose her own brush, her own canvas, her own subject. The girls at The Study are doing the same thing with different tools. CC0*

Last year's class built remarkable things on Rosie – and then graduated. That is how schools work. The projects do not compound the way they do in a company. What compounds is the posture.

The Study's Framework describes this posture as design thinking methodology: students "iterate, experiment, and learn through failure within a structured and supportive environment, enabling them to challenge assumptions and pursue unconventional solutions."[^8] The tools change every year. The methodology does not.

Tool fluency means: the students in this article will use a tool next year that does not exist yet. The class after them will replace it again. Some will build their own. This is what Imagination Age literacy looks like. Not loyalty to a platform. The ability to choose one, adapt one, or create one – fluently, fearlessly, on the merits.

[AMALIA QUOTE – on what changed in students once they started building with AI, not just using it. To be captured in interview.]

For policymakers, the contrast with the age-gating approach could not be sharper. The banning framework treats AI tools as threats to be managed. Tool fluency treats them as instruments to be mastered. The difference in outcome is the difference between a generation of cautious consumers and a generation of confident creators.

Seventy-three percent of Canadian students are already using generative AI for schoolwork.[^6] The question is not whether they will use it. The question is whether anyone is teaching them to use it well.

## What You Can Do

This section is a playbook – four moves for any school, board, or parent who reads this and wants to start.

**Own your foundation. Free everything above it.** A school's AI strategy has two layers. The foundation (institutional AI, data governance, operational infrastructure) should be sovereign. Everything above it (the tools students choose for their own projects, the agents and tools they build themselves) should be open. The job is not to pick the tool. The job is to build the floor solid enough that students can stand on it and build anything.

**Let students build, with the tools they pick.** The most powerful curriculum is the one students write themselves. The Imagination Age does not reward students who memorized the right answer. It rewards students who can ask a better question.

**Build the method, not just the tools.** Tools without a method produce chaos – in classrooms and in companies. An AI consultant in Canada recently walked away from a three-year client relationship after watching the non-technical CEO open seven coding windows simultaneously, push untested changes directly to production, and break the systems the consultant had spent years building.[^10] The tools were identical to the ones the consultant used every day. The difference was the method. The Study understood this from the start: Rosie is not just an AI platform. She is the first step in a structured workflow – *poka-yoke* applied to education.[^11] Quality gates, human review, and teacher oversight are built into the architecture, not bolted on afterward. The Study's Framework calls it "a structured and supportive environment." In manufacturing, Shigeo Shingo called it mistake-proofing. The principle is the same: design the process so that the safe path is also the easy path.

**Make girls' voices the default, not the diversity slide.** The Information Age was built (mostly) by men. The Imagination Age is being built right now. The next decade of AI will be shaped by the people in the room when it is made. Whatever school you support, whatever board you sit on – the question is not whether girls are interested in AI. They are. The question is whether you have built a place where they can imagine with it.

## 42

There is a number on The Study's IT directr's project site – 42. It comes from *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*: 42 is the answer to life, the universe, and everything. The joke is that no one ever figured out the question.

The Information Age was the same. A century of gathering answers – the tools, the models, the infrastructure. We have them now. The question is what we will imagine with them.

The people most likely to find that question are the students in classrooms right now, building things nobody asked them to build, choosing tools nobody chose for them, solving problems nobody had thought to name. They have been doing this at The Study for decades. The only thing that changed is the power of the tools.

Don't panic. The future is in good hands.

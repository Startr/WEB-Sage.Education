Merriam-Webster named "slop" its 2025 Word of the Year: digital content of low quality, produced in large amounts by AI.[^1] The internet is full of it. But the problem was never the AI tools. The problem was that nobody taught anyone how to use them.

This article is the method.

It works whether you are a student writing a research paper, a professional drafting an article, or a creator building a newsletter. The tools you use will change. The five stages will not.

## The Five Stages

### Stage One: Research and Workshop

You start with a concept. Not a finished idea. A direction.

Your AI helps you research: pulling sources, finding data, identifying what has already been said about the topic. Then you workshop the angle together, through conversation. You push back on the AI. You ask it to find counterarguments. You test whether your idea holds up.

A student might do this in ChatGPT: "Find me three credible sources that disagree with my thesis." A professional might use Perplexity, which searches the web and attaches sources to every answer, then move into Claude to stress-test the argument.

The critical move at this stage is the one that slop producers skip entirely: deciding what the piece is *about*. Not the topic. The argument. What are you trying to say, and why should anyone care?

### Stage Two: The Pitch

Before you write a single word, lay out the structure. What is the thesis? What sources will you use? What order will the sections follow? What is the working title?

Think of this as the blueprint inspection before construction begins. You would not build a house without a plan. You should not write an article without one either.

In a simple workflow, this is an outline you write with the AI and revise until it is right. In a more advanced workflow, it becomes a document that the AI references throughout drafting.

### Stage Three: The Draft

Now the AI writes, following the structure you approved. It follows the tone, the reading level, the style you set. But it writes a first draft, not a final product.

The difference matters. A first draft is raw material shaped by your intent. Slop is raw material shaped by nothing.

### Stage Four: The Editorial Pass

This is where the article becomes yours.

You read what the AI wrote. You rewrite the sentences that sound generic. You cut the parts that add nothing. You add the details that only you could know: the personal experience, the specific example, the observation that came from your own thinking.

The article belongs to you not because you typed every word, but because you made every decision about which words stayed, which were cut, and what was added.

This stage is the one that separates published writing from slop. Skip it, and you are publishing someone else's homework. Do it, and the work is unambiguously yours.

### Stage Five: Publishing

The article moves from draft to live. This might mean pasting it into a blog editor, sending it as a newsletter, or submitting it as an assignment.

Before you publish, ask yourself three questions:

Is this specific? Does it name real people, real places, real numbers? Or could it be about anything?

Is this sourced? Can a reader verify what you are claiming? Or are you asking them to trust a machine?

Is this worth someone's time? Would you read this if someone else wrote it?

If the answer to all three is yes, publish it. If not, go back to Stage Four.

## The Staircase

The method works at every level because the AI tools exist on a spectrum. You go as far as your comfort and your tools allow.

### Entry Level: AI Search Tools

Start with a tool like Perplexity. It searches the web and gives you answers with sources attached. You do not need any technical knowledge. You just ask questions and get cited answers.

A student writing a term paper can use Perplexity to find three credible sources in two minutes instead of forty. That is Stage One of the method: the research phase, done with a tool anyone can use.

### Middle Level: AI Conversation Tools

Move up to ChatGPT, Claude, or [Sage.is](https://sage.is) in conversation mode. Here, the AI does not just search. It thinks with you. You bring your concept and your research. The AI helps you structure your argument, draft sections, suggest transitions, and find weaknesses.

You work section by section, keeping control of the argument while the AI handles the mechanical labour of first-draft prose. This covers Stages One through Three.

### Advanced Level: AI Agents

At the top of the staircase are agents: AI systems that can carry out multi-step tasks on their own.[^2] Research agents can pull and organize dozens of sources into structured briefs. Writing agents can draft to a style guide, run quality checks, and file the output for your review.

This covers the full pipeline, with you stepping in at Stage Four for the editorial pass that makes the work yours. The agent builds the scaffolding. You make the decisions.

## The Quality Gate

Here is the secret that most people miss: there should be a checkpoint between drafting and publishing. Professional newsrooms have editors. Publishing houses have reviewers. Academic journals have peer review. But the person using ChatGPT to write their company blog usually has nothing.

Quality gates can be simple. Read your draft aloud. If three sentences in a row start with "The," rewrite them. If no human being is named anywhere in the piece, it is probably not worth reading. If the piece could describe any company in any industry by changing three words, it is slop.

Quality gates can also be automated. At [Sage.is](https://sage.is) and [Sage.Education](https://sage.education), every article passes through a scripted audit that measures sentence variety, paragraph diversity, passive voice, and whether the piece actually names specific sources.[^3] These scripts do not replace your judgment. They catch the patterns your eyes miss on a third read-through.

The slop producers build no gate at all. They prompt, they accept, they publish. That absence of friction is what they are selling. But friction, applied at the right moment, is what makes writing worth reading.

## Why This Matters Legally

Most people do not know this: if you type a prompt into ChatGPT and publish whatever it gives you, you may not legally own the result.

In 2023, a U.S. federal judge ruled that works created by AI alone cannot receive copyright protection.[^4] The court said that typing a prompt is not creative control. If your only contribution is describing what you want and accepting what the machine produces, the output belongs to no one.

The five-stage method solves this problem naturally. A writer who shapes the thesis, approves the structure, rewrites the draft, and makes editorial decisions at every stage has exercised creative control. The work is theirs, legally and intellectually, because the method required them to make it theirs.

Slop belongs to no one. Work produced with a method belongs to you.

## Try It

Pick something you need to write this week. A paper. A blog post. An email that matters.

Open your AI tool of choice. But before you type a prompt, decide: what is this piece about? Not the topic. The argument. What are you trying to say?

Then follow the five stages. Research. Pitch. Draft. Edit. Publish.

Read the draft the AI gives you. Rewrite the parts that sound like everyone. Add the parts that sound like you. Check the facts. Name your sources.

Then publish it.

The AI did the labour. You did the thinking. The result is not slop. It is yours.
